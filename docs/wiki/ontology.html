What Ontology Brings to AI

Ontology is the philosophical study of what exists and how entities are classified and related. In logic and knowledge representation, an ontology provides the vocabulary of types, properties, and relations that a system can use to talk about the world. Without a stable ontology, even precise reasoning quickly degenerates into ambiguity.

In human practice, ontologies appear as taxonomies (such as biological species), legal categories, and common-sense distinctions like “physical object”, “event”, or “institution”. For an AI system, the ontology is not just an abstract list; it shapes how concepts are encoded, which dimensions are reserved for “facts” versus “values”, and how rules can be applied consistently across cases.

Ontology in Knowledge Representation

Formal ontologies in AI—such as those inspired by OWL, description logics, or domain-specific vocabularies—provide machine-readable definitions of concepts and their relations. They enable tasks like subsumption checking (“Dog IS_A Animal”), disjointness (“MagicZone DISJOINT_WITH CityX”), and property constraints.

However, classical ontology languages often assume crisp, symbolic categories. In AGISystem2, ontological distinctions must survive translation into high-dimensional conceptual space, where each concept becomes a region and relations become geometric operations. The ontology therefore guides both the symbolic layer and the geometric one.

Ontology in AGISystem2

AGISystem2 explicitly separates ontological dimensions (those that describe “what the world is like”) from axiological ones (those that capture values, permissions, or duties). The ontology partition defines which coordinates are reserved for structural, factual information: shapes, physical quantities, roles, causal relations, and so on.

The dimension catalogue enumerates these axes and associates them with names, ranges, and intended interpretations. Default relations (such as IS_A, PART_OF, CAUSES) are defined on top of this ontology and mapped to deterministic permutations so that the geometric engine can manipulate them consistently.

Initial “base theories” provide root facts and constraints that express the core ontology for a deployment: which concepts exist, which are disjoint, and which regions of the conceptual space are reserved or unavailable. This gives the system a stable backbone against which new facts, rules, and theories can be evaluated.

Philosophical and Practical Implications

By treating ontology as an explicit, versioned artefact, AGISystem2 makes it possible to audit and refine “how the system sees the world”. Misclassifications, missing categories, or overly coarse distinctions can be corrected by updating the ontology rather than by scattering ad-hoc fixes throughout the code.

This separation also clarifies the boundary between facts and values. Ontology anchors the factual side of reasoning; axiological layers operate on top, without rewriting what the system takes to exist. For users, this makes explanations clearer: they can see which parts of a decision stem from factual structure and which from policy or ethics.
