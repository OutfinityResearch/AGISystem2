Facts and Questions

Facts and questions are the foundation of every interaction with AGISystem2. They are written as short, flat sentences in a constrained dialect: a subject, a relation, and an object. This chapter shows all the shapes that the engine accepts and gives concrete examples, with code blocks separated clearly from the surrounding explanations.

Facts: Subject REL Object

The simplest unit is a fact. A fact is a single line that states a subject–relation–object triple. The subject is usually a short identifier such as Dog, Water, ProcedureX, or Alice. The relation is an upper-case verb or verb phrase such as IS_A, HAS_PROPERTY, REQUIRES, PROHIBITED_BY, PERMITTED_BY, CAUSES, or LOCATED_IN. The object is everything that follows the relation on the same line; it may be a single token or a short phrase. The line does not end in a question mark.

Basic typing and taxonomy facts:

Dog IS_A Animal
Water IS_A Substance
Alice IS_A Human

Property facts, where the object encodes an attribute or value:

Water HAS_PROPERTY boiling_point=100
Steel HAS_PROPERTY melting_point=1538C
Patient123 HAS_PROPERTY diagnosis=Hypertension

Deontic and regulatory facts, which assign permissions or prohibitions:

ExportData PROHIBITED_BY GDPR
ExportData PERMITTED_BY HIPAA
SurgeryX REQUIRES Consent
SurgeryX REQUIRES AuditTrail

Causal and temporal facts:

Fire CAUSES Smoke
Smoke CAUSED_BY Fire
Inspection BEFORE Shipment
Shipment AFTER Inspection

Spatial and narrative facts:

Alice LOCATED_IN CityX
CityX DISJOINT_WITH MagicZone
Alice CASTS Magic
SciFi_TechMagic PERMITS Magic_IN CityX

From the engine’s perspective these are all just triples, but the choice of relation name determines how reasoning modules interpret them. The Relations and Reasoning chapters describe those semantics; here the important point is that the syntax is always “Subject REL Object” on a single line.

Questions: Interrogatives and Canonical Triples

Questions reuse the same ingredients but appear as yes/no queries. There are two equivalent forms, both accepted by the engine.

The first form is a simple English interrogative. It is normalised by the TranslatorBridge and parsed into an IS_A triple:

Is Dog an Animal?
Is Alice Human?
Is Water a Substance?

The second form is a canonical triple followed by a question mark. This works for any relation, not just IS_A:

Water HAS_PROPERTY boiling_point=100?
Alice LOCATED_IN CityX?
ExportData PROHIBITED_BY GDPR?
SurgeryX REQUIRES Consent?
Fire CAUSES Smoke?
Alice CASTS Magic?

In both forms the parser ends up with the same internal representation: a subject, a relation, and an object, plus a flag indicating that this is a query. The ask method on EngineAPI and the ask CLI command expect exactly these shapes.

Counterfactual Questions

Counterfactual queries look like ordinary questions paired with extra facts. The syntax for the question itself does not change; it is still an interrogative or a canonical triple with a question mark. The additional assumptions are written as normal fact lines and are passed separately.

For example, in the CLI you might write:

cf Water HAS_PROPERTY boiling_point=50? | Water HAS_PROPERTY boiling_point=50

At API level the same idea appears as:

question   = "Water HAS_PROPERTY boiling_point=50?"
extraFacts = ["Water HAS_PROPERTY boiling_point=50"]

The key rule is that counterfactuals do not introduce new syntax: they reuse facts and questions exactly as described above. The engine never accepts nested formulas or free-form paragraphs; everything is expressed as flat triples or their interrogative counterparts.

Summary

Every fact that AGISystem2 stores and every question it answers is written as a single subject–relation–object triple or as a small English question that normalises to such a triple. If you keep this pattern in mind, all other syntactic features—such as theory files and macros—can be seen as ways of grouping or manipulating these triples rather than as separate languages.
