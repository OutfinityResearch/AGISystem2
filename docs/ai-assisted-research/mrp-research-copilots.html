Toward an MRP Theory of Research Copilots

Orchestrating interpretive regimes in scientific AI.

Overview

Scientific research is better described as an organized succession of local interpretive regimes than as a single homogeneous reasoning process. This article proposes a compact theoretical framework for research copilots grounded in Meta-Rational Pragmatics (MRP). Current developments in AI co-scientists [GOTTWEIS-ETAL-2025], self-driving laboratories [TOM-ETAL-2024], and automated research pipelines [LU-ETAL-2026] suggest that several scientific sub-tasks are already partially automatable. However, robust end-to-end scientific reasoning remains elusive, in part because generic language models struggle with verification, causal reasoning, and domain-specific epistemic standards [SON-ETAL-2025]. The MRP framework proposes that a research copilot should not be modeled as a generic scientific agent, but as a regime-sensitive orchestration system that identifies the current mode of inquiry, mobilizes domain-specific knowledge, and validates intermediate results according to the criteria of the current regime.

The Heterogeneity of Scientific Work

Contemporary AI for science assumes segments of the scientific workflow can be partially automated, including literature synthesis [ASAI-2026], hypothesis generation [GOTTWEIS-ETAL-2025], and experimental selection [ZHANG-ETAL-2024]. Yet, success in one segment does not automatically transfer to another. Retrieval-augmented systems excel at synthesis, but verification of scientific claims remains substantially harder [SON-ETAL-2025]. Similarly, closed-loop automation works well in experimental settings with narrow optimization targets [TOM-ETAL-2024], but this is not equivalent to cross-domain theoretical judgment [ZHANG-ETAL-2025]. This uneven competence arises because scientific work is operationally heterogeneous; research is not a monolithic activity but a collection of distinct epistemic burdens.

Interpretive Regimes

We introduce the notion of interpretive regime to capture these local differences without reifying them into disconnected faculties. A regime is a bounded mode of inquiry with its own epistemic objects, transformation rules, and validation procedures. Research proceeds through several such regimes (e.g., Observational, Model-constructive, Deductive-formal, Experimental-interventional, Integrative), and a scientific result often draws authority from multiple regimes simultaneously. A pattern discovered under exploratory criteria is not yet a causal explanation; a plausible literature synthesis is not yet a validated conclusion. The point is not that science is fragmented, but that its rational discipline is locally structured.

Orchestration Architecture

A research copilot should be designed as a regime-sensitive system where local interpreters operate under distinct admissibility conditions and exchange results through controlled transitions. This architecture is consistent with modern agent systems [OPENAI-AGENTS-2026] where tool execution, memory, and validation are separated rather than collapsed into a single monolithic prompt. The copilot requires an operational account of what kind of artifact is being manipulated, what transformations are licit, and what validator is competent to judge the result.

Domain knowledge in this framework is not merely a semantic store of facts. It includes measurement conventions, accepted model classes, standard artifacts, and canonical ontologies. A mathematics copilot and a biology copilot differ in the relative roles of proof, observation, uncertainty, and mechanism. The architectural goal is thus a shared meta-pragmatic core combined with discipline-specific interpreters and validators.

Reliability and Evaluation

The framework changes how research copilots should be evaluated. Success is not merely the production of a plausible hypothesis or paper, but whether the system selected appropriate regimes, maintained provenance across transitions, used validators appropriate to local tasks, and failed visibly when a transition was under-justified. This evaluation focus is necessary because local plausibility and scientific adequacy remain importantly distinct [SON-ETAL-2025]. The immediate research task is to represent scientific knowledge and intermediate artifacts in machine-tractable forms and to develop explicit, regime-specific validators that go beyond retrospective natural-language checking.
