Interpretive Regimes and Scientific Reliability

Why local standards matter for automated inquiry.

Overview

Scientific work is governed by local standards. Treating scientific reasoning as a monolithic language process hides the epistemic heterogeneity of research. This article argues that to build reliable scientific AI, we must make these local interpretive regimes explicit and develop validation procedures tailored to the epistemic burdens of each regime. Current end-to-end automation systems [LU-ETAL-2026] excel at generating plausible output, but they frequently fail at the critical checking required for scientific adequacy [SON-ETAL-2025]. The failure is structural: when scientific reasoning is collapsed into generic continuation, the discipline required to translate results across different interpretive boundaries is lost.

The Internal Heterogeneity of Science

Scientific practice is often described in broad phases like observation, hypothesis, experiment, and theory. While useful, this vocabulary is too coarse for computational purposes. What matters computationally is the local criteria governing what counts as a relevant object, what counts as an admissible operation, and what kind of check is sufficient at that stage. A pattern discovered through exploratory criteria is not yet a causal explanation [ZHANG-ETAL-2025]; a plausible literature synthesis is not yet a validated result [ASAI-2026]. Each stage of scientific work has different admissibility criteria for evidence, transformations, and conclusions. Generic AI agents that collapse these distinctions fail because they do not respect the operational structure of scientific discipline.

The Validation Crisis in Automated Research

The core challenge in contemporary automated research is not hypothesis generation, but hypothesis verification. Empirical evidence suggests that local plausibility and scientific adequacy remain importantly distinct [SON-ETAL-2025]. Automated systems often produce results that are linguistically clear but scientifically fragile because they lack explicit constraint tracking. This is a validation crisis: as AI systems participate in hypothesis, interpretation, and review, failures of provenance and reproducibility become increasingly consequential. The reliance on retrospective natural-language checking as the primary validator is insufficient for maintaining scientific rigour [ECKMANN-2026].

Regime-Specific Validation

Validation procedures cannot be outsourced entirely to LLMs. A research copilot must utilize regime-specific validators that match the nature of the inquiry: formal consistency checkers for deductive-formal stages, causal discrimination engines for experimental-interventional stages, and robust metadata/provenance auditors for observational stages. Explicit validation is a prerequisite for scientific adequacy. If an AI system operates within a model-constructive regime, its output must be validated against the constraints of that model class, rather than just linguistic coherence.

Scientific Reliability

The reliability of a scientific trajectory depends on whether intermediate artifacts remain meaningful when translated across regimes. A copilot becomes scientifically useful when it manages these transitions, tracks provenance, and alerts the user when a transition is under-justified. This is the path to truly trustworthy scientific automation. Scientific AI must transition from a model of generic generation to one of governed orchestration, where validity is enforced locally at each step of the inquiry process. True scientific reliability, in this view, is a distributed property enforced by explicit validators, not a monolithic outcome of model training.
