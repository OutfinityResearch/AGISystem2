# Psychology Theory - Simple Facts (One Sentence Each)

# BASIC EMOTIONS
Joy is a basic emotion characterized by smiling and positive feelings.
Sadness is a basic emotion involving frowning and withdrawal.
Fear is a basic emotion that triggers fight-or-flight response for survival.
Anger is a basic emotion involving increased blood pressure and confrontation.
Disgust is a basic emotion that helps avoid contamination and disease.
Surprise is a brief neutral emotion that orients attention.

# COMPLEX EMOTIONS
Pride is a complex emotion requiring self-evaluation and achievement recognition.
Shame is a complex emotion focusing on the entire self and causing hiding.
Guilt is a complex emotion focusing on specific actions and motivating repair.
Jealousy is a complex emotion involving three parties and relationship threat.
Envy is wanting what someone else has.
Embarrassment is distress from social exposure.
Love is attachment bonding involving oxytocin and dopamine.
Gratitude is appreciation for benefits received.
Hope is positive expectation for the future.
Anxiety is worry about future threats.
Grief is prolonged sadness from loss.

# EMOTION THEORIES
James-Lange theory says bodily changes cause emotions.
Cannon-Bard theory says emotions and bodily changes occur simultaneously.
Schachter-Singer theory says emotion requires arousal plus cognitive label.
Lazarus theory says cognitive appraisal determines emotion.
Facial feedback hypothesis says facial expressions influence emotional experience.

# EMOTION REGULATION
Reappraisal is cognitive reframing before emotion peaks.
Suppression is hiding expression after emotion arises.
Distraction shifts attention away from emotion triggers.
Acceptance involves allowing emotions without judgment.
Emotional intelligence includes perceiving, understanding, managing, and using emotions.

# MEMORY TYPES
Sensory memory lasts less than one second with large capacity.
Short-term memory lasts 15-30 seconds with capacity of seven items.
Working memory actively processes information using multiple components.
Long-term memory has unlimited duration and capacity.
Episodic memory stores personal experiences and events.
Semantic memory stores facts and general knowledge.
Procedural memory stores skills and habits without conscious awareness.
Implicit memory operates unconsciously.
Explicit memory requires conscious recall.

# MEMORY PROCESSES
Encoding converts sensory input to memory.
Deep semantic processing leads to better retention than shallow processing.
Consolidation stabilizes memories and requires sleep.
Storage maintains information over time.
Retrieval accesses stored memories using cues.
Forgetting occurs through decay or interference.
Reconsolidation makes retrieved memories temporarily malleable.

# ATTENTION
Selective attention focuses on specific stimuli while filtering others.
Divided attention attempts to handle multiple tasks simultaneously.
Sustained attention maintains vigilance over prolonged periods.
Inattentional blindness causes missing obvious stimuli when attention is focused elsewhere.
The cocktail party effect enables focusing on one conversation in noise.

# LEARNING
Classical conditioning pairs neutral stimuli with unconditioned stimuli.
Operant conditioning modifies behavior through consequences.
Reinforcement increases behavior frequency.
Punishment decreases behavior frequency.
Positive reinforcement adds pleasant stimuli.
Negative reinforcement removes unpleasant stimuli.
Observational learning occurs by watching others.
Latent learning occurs without immediate demonstration.

# PERCEPTION
Bottom-up processing flows from sensation to cognition.
Top-down processing uses expectations to interpret sensations.
Depth perception enables seeing three dimensions from two-dimensional images.
Binocular cues for depth include retinal disparity and convergence.
Monocular cues for depth include linear perspective and interposition.
Gestalt principles describe how we organize visual information.
Perceptual constancy maintains stable perception despite changing input.

# DECISION MAKING AND PROBLEM SOLVING
Algorithms guarantee correct solutions but are slow.
Heuristics are mental shortcuts that are fast but risk errors.
Availability heuristic judges frequency by ease of recall.
Representativeness heuristic judges category by similarity to prototype.
Anchoring heuristic relies too heavily on initial information.
Confirmation bias favors information confirming existing beliefs.
Fundamental attribution error overestimates dispositional causes.
Self-serving bias attributes success internally and failure externally.
Insight involves sudden realization of solutions.
Functional fixedness limits seeing alternative uses for objects.

# LANGUAGE
Phonology deals with speech sounds.
Morphology deals with word structure.
Syntax deals with sentence structure.
Semantics deals with meaning.
Pragmatics deals with contextual language use.
Language acquisition may involve a critical period.
Chomsky proposed an innate Language Acquisition Device.
Overregularization shows children learning grammatical rules.

# ANXIETY DISORDERS
Generalized Anxiety Disorder involves excessive worry about multiple domains.
Panic Disorder features recurrent unexpected panic attacks.
Social Anxiety Disorder involves fear of social scrutiny.
Specific Phobia involves excessive fear of particular objects or situations.
Agoraphobia involves fear of situations where escape is difficult.

# MOOD DISORDERS
Major Depressive Disorder involves at least two weeks of depressed mood or anhedonia.
Persistent Depressive Disorder is chronic low-grade depression.
Bipolar I Disorder requires at least one manic episode.
Bipolar II Disorder involves hypomanic and depressive episodes.
Manic episodes involve elevated mood, grandiosity, and decreased sleep need.
Depressive episodes involve sadness, anhedonia, and fatigue.

# SCHIZOPHRENIA SPECTRUM
Schizophrenia involves positive symptoms like delusions and hallucinations.
Schizophrenia also involves negative symptoms like flat affect and avolition.
Schizoaffective Disorder combines psychotic and mood symptoms.
Delusions are false fixed beliefs.
Hallucinations are perceptions without external stimuli.
The dopamine hypothesis links schizophrenia to dopamine dysregulation.

# PERSONALITY DISORDERS
Borderline Personality Disorder involves unstable relationships and emotions.
Antisocial Personality Disorder involves disregard for others and lack of remorse.
Narcissistic Personality Disorder involves grandiosity and need for admiration.
Avoidant Personality Disorder involves social inhibition and feelings of inadequacy.
Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder involves perfectionism and rigidity.
Personality disorders show pervasive maladaptive patterns.

# TRAUMA-RELATED DISORDERS
PTSD requires trauma exposure and includes intrusion, avoidance, negative cognitions, and arousal symptoms.
Acute Stress Disorder is similar to PTSD but lasts less than one month.
Flashbacks are vivid reliving of traumatic experiences.
Hypervigilance is heightened alertness for threats.
EMDR is an effective PTSD treatment.

# OCD AND RELATED DISORDERS
Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder involves intrusive thoughts and repetitive behaviors.
Obsessions are unwanted intrusive thoughts causing anxiety.
Compulsions are repetitive behaviors reducing anxiety temporarily.
Body Dysmorphic Disorder involves preoccupation with perceived appearance flaws.
Hoarding Disorder involves difficulty discarding possessions.
Exposure and Response Prevention is effective OCD treatment.

# NEURODEVELOPMENTAL DISORDERS
Autism Spectrum Disorder involves social communication deficits and restricted behaviors.
ADHD involves inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity.
Intellectual Disability involves deficits in intellectual and adaptive functioning.
Specific Learning Disorder involves difficulties in reading, writing, or math.
Early intervention improves outcomes for neurodevelopmental disorders.

# EATING DISORDERS
Anorexia Nervosa involves severe food restriction and fear of weight gain.
Bulimia Nervosa involves binge eating and compensatory behaviors.
Binge Eating Disorder involves recurrent binge eating without compensation.
Eating disorders have the highest mortality rate among psychiatric disorders.
Body image distortion is central to eating disorders.

# DISSOCIATIVE DISORDERS
Dissociative Identity Disorder involves two or more distinct identities.
Dissociative Amnesia involves inability to recall important information.
Depersonalization involves feeling detached from oneself.
Derealization involves feeling that surroundings are unreal.
Dissociation often follows severe trauma.

# SUBSTANCE USE DISORDERS
Substance Use Disorder involves impaired control and continued use despite harm.
Tolerance requires increasing amounts to achieve effects.
Withdrawal involves negative symptoms when substance use stops.
Addiction affects brain reward pathways.
Relapse is common in substance use disorders.

# DISORDER MODELS
The biopsychosocial model integrates biological, psychological, and social factors.
The diathesis-stress model requires both vulnerability and stress triggers.
The biological model emphasizes genetics and neurobiology.
The cognitive model focuses on maladaptive thought patterns.
The behavioral model emphasizes learned associations.

# PIAGET'S STAGES
The sensorimotor stage involves learning through senses and actions.
The preoperational stage involves symbolic thought but illogical reasoning.
The concrete operational stage enables logical thinking about concrete situations.
The formal operational stage enables abstract and hypothetical thinking.
Object permanence develops during the sensorimotor stage.
Conservation understanding develops during concrete operations.
Egocentrism characterizes preoperational thinking.

# ERIKSON'S STAGES
Trust vs. Mistrust occurs in infancy and depends on caregiver responsiveness.
Autonomy vs. Shame occurs in toddlerhood and involves independence.
Initiative vs. Guilt occurs in preschool and involves taking initiative.
Industry vs. Inferiority occurs in school age and involves competence.
Identity vs. Role Confusion occurs in adolescence and involves identity formation.
Intimacy vs. Isolation occurs in young adulthood and involves forming relationships.
Generativity vs. Stagnation occurs in middle age and involves contributing to society.
Integrity vs. Despair occurs in old age and involves life review.

# ATTACHMENT
Bowlby's attachment theory describes infant-caregiver bonding.
Secure attachment results from responsive caregiving.
Avoidant attachment results from rejecting caregiving.
Ambivalent attachment results from inconsistent caregiving.
Disorganized attachment results from frightening caregiving.
The Strange Situation assesses attachment styles.
Attachment styles tend to persist into adulthood.
Internal working models are cognitive representations of relationships.

# COGNITIVE DEVELOPMENT
Theory of Mind is understanding that others have mental states.
False-belief tasks assess Theory of Mind.
Theory of Mind emerges around age 4.
Conservation is understanding that quantity remains constant despite appearance.
Reversibility is mentally undoing actions.
Classification involves organizing objects into categories.
Seriation involves ordering objects along dimensions.

# LANGUAGE DEVELOPMENT
Babbling begins around 4-6 months.
First words appear around 12 months.
Vocabulary explosion occurs after 18 months.
Two-word stage occurs at 18-24 months.
Telegraphic speech omits non-essential words.
Overregularization shows rule learning.
A critical period may exist for language learning.

# MORAL DEVELOPMENT
Kohlberg proposed three levels of moral reasoning.
Preconventional morality focuses on punishment and self-interest.
Conventional morality focuses on social approval and law.
Postconventional morality focuses on universal principles.
Moral reasoning develops with cognitive abilities.
Gilligan proposed an alternative care-based approach.

# AGING
Fluid intelligence declines with age.
Crystallized intelligence maintains or improves with age.
Processing speed decreases with aging.
Wisdom may increase with age and experience.
Dementia involves severe cognitive decline.
Alzheimer's disease is the most common form of dementia.

# BEHAVIORISM
Behaviorism focuses on observable behavior.
Watson founded behaviorism.
Skinner developed operant conditioning.
Little Albert demonstrated classical conditioning of fear.
Behavior modification applies conditioning principles.
Behaviorism rejects studying mental states.

# PSYCHOANALYSIS
Freud founded psychoanalysis.
The unconscious contains repressed thoughts and desires.
The id operates on the pleasure principle.
The ego operates on the reality principle.
The superego represents internalized moral standards.
Defense mechanisms protect against anxiety.
Free association reveals unconscious content.
Dream analysis interprets symbolic meanings.

# HUMANISM
Humanism emphasizes personal growth and potential.
Maslow proposed a hierarchy of needs.
Self-actualization is realizing one's full potential.
Rogers developed client-centered therapy.
Unconditional positive regard accepts clients without judgment.
Humanism emphasizes free will and choice.

# COGNITIVE PSYCHOLOGY
Cognitive psychology studies mental processes.
The information processing model compares mind to computer.
Cognitive psychology emerged as cognitive revolution.
Mental representations include schemas and scripts.
Cognitive processes include attention, memory, and reasoning.

# BIOLOGICAL PSYCHOLOGY
Biological psychology studies brain-behavior relationships.
Neurotransmitters are chemical messengers in the brain.
Dopamine is involved in reward and movement.
Serotonin regulates mood and sleep.
The amygdala processes emotions, especially fear.
The hippocampus is critical for memory formation.
The prefrontal cortex handles executive functions.
Brain imaging reveals neural activity.

# PERSONALITY THEORIES
The Big Five traits are Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism.
Traits show consistency across situations and time.
Social cognitive theory emphasizes reciprocal determinism.
Self-efficacy is belief in one's capability.
Locus of control is belief about control over events.
Person-situation interaction shapes behavior.

# MOTIVATION
Intrinsic motivation comes from internal interest.
Extrinsic motivation comes from external rewards.
Maslow's hierarchy ranges from physiological to self-actualization needs.
Self-determination theory emphasizes autonomy, competence, and relatedness.
Drive reduction theory says motivation reduces biological needs.
Arousal theory says we seek optimal stimulation levels.

# SOCIAL PSYCHOLOGY
Attribution theory explains how we determine causes of behavior.
The fundamental attribution error overestimates personality factors.
Cognitive dissonance is discomfort from inconsistent beliefs.
Conformity is matching behavior to group norms.
Asch's experiments demonstrated conformity in line judgments.
Obedience is following authority commands.
Milgram's experiments showed high obedience rates.
Social facilitation improves performance on easy tasks.
Social loafing reduces individual effort in groups.
Groupthink is flawed decision-making in cohesive groups.
Bystander effect reduces helping when others are present.
Diffusion of responsibility spreads accountability.

# ATTITUDES AND PERSUASION
Attitudes have cognitive, affective, and behavioral components.
Cognitive dissonance motivates attitude change.
The central route to persuasion uses logical arguments.
The peripheral route to persuasion uses superficial cues.
Foot-in-the-door technique starts with small requests.
Door-in-the-face technique starts with large requests.

# STEREOTYPES AND PREJUDICE
Stereotypes are generalized beliefs about groups.
Prejudice is negative attitude toward group members.
Discrimination is behavioral expression of prejudice.
In-group bias favors one's own group.
Out-group homogeneity sees out-group members as similar.
Implicit bias operates unconsciously.

# THERAPY APPROACHES
Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy targets thoughts and behaviors.
Psychodynamic therapy explores unconscious conflicts.
Humanistic therapy emphasizes growth and potential.
Behavior therapy applies conditioning principles.
Exposure therapy confronts feared stimuli.
Systematic desensitization gradually reduces fear.
Cognitive restructuring challenges irrational thoughts.
Mindfulness-based therapies emphasize present awareness.
Group therapy provides peer support.
Family therapy addresses family system issues.

# STRESS AND COPING
Stress is response to demanding situations.
Stressors can be acute or chronic.
The fight-or-flight response prepares for danger.
The General Adaptation Syndrome has alarm, resistance, exhaustion phases.
Chronic stress harms physical and mental health.
Problem-focused coping addresses the stressor directly.
Emotion-focused coping manages emotional reactions.
Social support buffers against stress.
Resilience is ability to recover from adversity.

# INTELLIGENCE
Intelligence is capacity for learning and problem solving.
IQ tests measure cognitive abilities.
The g factor represents general intelligence.
Multiple intelligences theory proposes distinct abilities.
Intelligence has genetic and environmental influences.
The Flynn effect shows rising IQ scores over time.
Emotional intelligence involves understanding and managing emotions.

# CONSCIOUSNESS
Consciousness is awareness of internal and external stimuli.
The unconscious processes information outside awareness.
Circadian rhythms regulate sleep-wake cycles.
REM sleep involves rapid eye movements and dreaming.
Non-REM sleep has four stages of increasing depth.
Sleep deprivation impairs cognitive and physical functioning.
Dreams may process emotions and consolidate memories.

# SENSATION AND PERCEPTION
Sensation is detecting physical energy.
Perception is interpreting sensory information.
Absolute threshold is minimum detectable stimulus.
Difference threshold is minimum detectable change.
Weber's law relates difference threshold to stimulus intensity.
Signal detection theory considers decision criteria.
Sensory adaptation reduces response to constant stimuli.

# MOTIVATION AND EMOTION INTEGRATION
Emotions motivate behavior toward or away from stimuli.
The Yerkes-Dodson law relates arousal to performance.
Optimal arousal level depends on task difficulty.
Facial expressions communicate emotions across cultures.
The two-factor theory requires arousal and cognition.
Emotion regulation strategies vary in effectiveness.

# RESEARCH METHODS
Experiments establish cause-effect relationships.
Random assignment controls for confounds.
Correlational studies identify relationships without causation.
Case studies provide detailed individual information.
Surveys gather self-report data from many participants.
Naturalistic observation studies behavior in natural settings.
Double-blind procedures prevent experimenter bias.
Statistical significance indicates unlikely results from chance.

# ETHICS IN PSYCHOLOGY
Informed consent requires voluntary participation with knowledge.
Confidentiality protects participant information.
Debriefing explains study purpose after participation.
Institutional Review Boards approve research protocols.
Animal research follows ethical guidelines.
Deception is minimized and explained in debriefing.
